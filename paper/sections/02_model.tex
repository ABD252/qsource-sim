\section{Model and methods}
\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Joint spectral amplitude}

We consider collinear, single-spatial-mode SPDC and describe the emitted
photon pair by its joint spectral amplitude (JSA). Writing $\nu_s$ and
$\nu_i$ for the angular-frequency detunings of signal and idler from
their central design frequencies, the JSA factorizes into an
energy-conservation term set by the pump and a momentum-conservation
term set by the crystal \cite{grice1997},
\begin{equation}
  f(\nu_s,\nu_i) = \alpha(\nu_s+\nu_i)\,\phi(\nu_s,\nu_i) .
  \label{eq:jsa}
\end{equation}
For a transform-limited Gaussian pump the envelope is
$\alpha(\nu) = \exp[-\nu^2/(2\sigma^2)]$, a band along the antidiagonal
$\nu_s+\nu_i=0$; $\sigma$ is the standard deviation of the amplitude
envelope in angular frequency. Bandwidths are quoted throughout as the
corresponding ordinary-frequency values; the reference pump has
$\sigma/2\pi = 0.164$~THz. For the phase matching we use the standard
first-order group-velocity-mismatch (GVM) expansion of the wave-vector
mismatch in a crystal of length $L$,
\begin{equation}
  \phi(\nu_s,\nu_i) = \mathrm{sinc}\!\left(\frac{\Delta k\,L}{2}\right),
  \qquad
  \Delta k = \kappa_s \nu_s + \kappa_i \nu_i ,
  \label{eq:pm}
\end{equation}
with $\mathrm{sinc}(x)=\sin(x)/x$ and
$\kappa_j = 1/v_g^{\mathrm{pump}} - 1/v_g^{(j)}$ the group-velocity
mismatch between the pump and photon $j$. The reference crystal has
$L = 10$~mm, $\kappa_s = +0.207$~ns/m, and $\kappa_i = -0.318$~ns/m,
representative of PPKTP pumped at 775~nm and producing degenerate pairs
at 1550~nm \cite{grice1997,uren2005,graffitti2018}. The ratio
$\kappa_s/\kappa_i$ sets the tilt of the sinc ridge in the
$(\nu_s,\nu_i)$ plane, and the interplay of this tilt with the pump
band determines how separable the JSA can be
(Fig.~\ref{fig:anatomy}). We deliberately retain only this first-order
model: no Sellmeier dispersion, no higher-order terms, and no
apodization of the nonlinearity \cite{branczyk2011,graffitti2018}, so
that the imperfection budgets below isolate pump effects at a fixed,
transparent phase-matching geometry.

\subsection{Schmidt decomposition}

The JSA is evaluated on a square grid of $n$ points per axis over a
window of half-width 4--8~THz (chosen per study so that the intensity
decays to zero well inside the boundary) and normalized so that
$\sum |f|^2 = 1$. The Schmidt decomposition of the discretized JSA is
its singular-value decomposition \cite{law2000}: with singular values
$s_k$, the Schmidt coefficients are
$\lambda_k = s_k^2 / \sum_j s_j^2$, and the heralded-photon spectral
purity and Schmidt number are
\begin{equation}
  P = \sum_k \lambda_k^2 , \qquad K = 1/P .
  \label{eq:schmidt}
\end{equation}
$P = 1$ ($K = 1$) corresponds to a fully separable JSA. At the
reference point $P = 0.8100$ ($K = 1.2345$).

\subsection{Imperfect pump as a mixed-state ensemble}

An imperfect pump is a classical ensemble of pure pump realizations,
so the heralded signal photon is described by a density matrix rather
than a single Schmidt spectrum. We Monte-Carlo sample $N = 120$ pure
realizations ($N = 1000$ for the RIN sweep, whose per-realization cost
is lower), compute the discretized JSA matrix $A_k$ for each, and
accumulate
\begin{equation}
  \rho \propto \sum_{k} w_k\, A_k A_k^\dagger ,
  \label{eq:rho}
\end{equation}
from which the heralded purity follows in a normalization-independent
form,
\begin{equation}
  P = \frac{\mathrm{Tr}(\rho^2)}{[\mathrm{Tr}(\rho)]^2} .
  \label{eq:purity}
\end{equation}
Each imperfection is parameterized directly by laser datasheet
statistics and enters only the pump. Per realization we sample:
(i) \emph{center jitter/drift} --- a Gaussian shift of the pump center
frequency with the quoted rms value, treated as a quasi-static
shot-to-shot ensemble; (ii) \emph{longitudinal multimode structure} ---
one comb line drawn from a set of $M$ discrete lines centered
symmetrically on the pump, at offsets $(m - (M-1)/2)$ times the mode
spacing for $m = 0,\dots,M-1$ (half-integer multiples of the spacing
when $M$ is even), with normalized line weights (uniform unless
stated); the total pump shift is the sum of
the comb-line offset and the jitter draw; and (iii) \emph{relative
intensity noise} (RIN) --- a realization weight
$w = \max[0,\, 1 + \mathcal{N}(0, r^2)]$, where $\mathcal{N}(0, r^2)$
is a zero-mean Gaussian with standard deviation $r$, the fractional rms
intensity noise, each realization's JSA being individually normalized.
The 10~mm jitter sweep, which has the largest Monte-Carlo variance, is
run with three independent seeds (7, 8, 9) to estimate sampling
uncertainty; the remaining Monte-Carlo sweeps use a single seed (7),
and for the RIN sweep the heralded purity is exactly independent of
the sampled weights (see the validation suite below).

\subsection{Numerical robustness and validation}

Ideal-pump sweeps use $n = 512$; Monte-Carlo studies use $n = 256$,
which is converged to better than $10^{-4}$ in $P$ against $n = 768$
at the hardest points of the parameter sweeps (0.05~THz bandwidth over
a 6~THz half-window; 2~mm crystal over an 8~THz half-window). The RIN
sweep uses a coarser $n = 64$ grid: there every realization shares the
identical JSA and only the scalar weights vary, so the reported purity
invariance is exact at any grid size. At
mid-curve jitter (0.25~THz rms) the three seeds give
$P = 0.484/0.460/0.513$, a spread of about $\pm 0.03$, while grid
refinement at the same point changes $P$ by only $\sim 2\times10^{-5}$:
Monte-Carlo noise, not discretization, dominates the error budget. The
code ships with a validation suite of nine physics tests, including
exact collapse of the zero-imperfection ensemble to the pure-state SVD
purity ($P = 0.8100$), exact RIN invariance of the heralded purity
(difference below $10^{-9}$), reduction of a comb with zero side-line
weight to the single-mode result, and monotonic degradation of purity
with jitter.

All simulations use the open-source \texttt{qsource-sim} package
(Python, MIT license); every figure and table in this paper regenerates
from a declarative YAML configuration file, one per study.
