\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Spontaneous parametric down-conversion (SPDC) remains the workhorse
process for generating photon pairs and, by detection of one photon of
a pair, heralded single photons; it underpinned the first two-photon
interference experiments \cite{hong1987} and the bright
polarization-entangled sources that followed \cite{kwiat1995}. For
modern applications the heralded photon must also be spectrally pure.
When the joint spectral amplitude (JSA) of the pair carries
signal--idler frequency correlations, the heralding measurement leaves
its partner in a mixed spectral state, and photons from independent
sources become partially distinguishable, degrading quantum
interference \cite{grice1997}. The Schmidt decomposition makes this
quantitative: the heralded purity is the inverse of the Schmidt number
of the JSA \cite{law2000}. Matching the group velocities of pump and
down-converted fields can render the JSA approximately factorable at
the source \cite{uren2005}, a strategy demonstrated experimentally with heralded
photons of approximately 95\% purity produced without lossy spectral
filtering \cite{mosley2008}.

A mature source-engineering literature has since pushed intrinsic
purity toward unity, most prominently by apodizing the nonlinear
response through domain engineering of the crystal
\cite{branczyk2011,graffitti2018}, and spectrally pure heralded sources
are now a standard requirement in single-photon-source design
\cite{meyerscott2020}. Cross-comparisons of purity-characterization
techniques on one and the same source have also shown that estimates
based on the joint spectral intensity alone, blind to the joint
spectral phase -- pump chirp in particular -- can substantially
overestimate the purity a real source achieves \cite{zielnicki2018}.

In much of this work the pump laser is idealized as a noiseless,
transform-limited, single-longitudinal-mode Gaussian, although the
idealization is not universal. Experiments with a multimode cw diode
pump have shown that the pump's coherence structure transfers to the
biphoton \cite{kwon2009}, and theory has traced how pump chirp maps
onto the chirp and purity of the heralded single photon
\cite{sanchezlozano2012}; the analogous transfer of partial pump
coherence in the spatial (rather than spectral) domain has likewise
been analyzed for the two-photon field \cite{jha2010}. These studies
establish pump coherence as a phenomenon that propagates into the
biphoton. What has been missing is the engineering translation: a
mapping from the specifications printed on a commercial laser
datasheet -- center-frequency drift and jitter, relative intensity
noise (RIN), and the longitudinal-mode structure of the laser cavity
-- to a quantitative purity budget with stated uncertainties.

This paper provides that translation for a concrete, numerically
transparent model. We study a collinear SPDC source described by
first-order group-velocity-mismatch (GVM) phase matching, with
parameters representative of a 10 mm PPKTP crystal pumped at 775 nm and
emitting at 1550 nm, with a transform-limited Gaussian pump of 0.164
THz bandwidth (standard deviation of the amplitude envelope); the
ideal heralded purity of this
reference design is $P = 0.810$. Each
pump imperfection is mapped to a Monte-Carlo ensemble of pure pump
realizations, and the heralded photon is described by the
ensemble-averaged density matrix, from which we compute the mixed-state
purity directly. The Monte-Carlo machinery is validated externally
against an exact closed-form purity for Gaussian phase matching, with
which it agrees within error bars (Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}).
The headline of the resulting imperfection budget
(Table~\ref{tab:budget}) is that center jitter of $27.7 \pm 0.3$,
$62.7 \pm 0.7$, and $91 \pm 1$ GHz rms (mean $\pm$ SEM over 16
Monte-Carlo seeds) consumes a 1\%, 5\%, and 10\% relative purity
budget, respectively -- the 1\% figure about one sixth of the 0.164
THz (164 GHz) pump bandwidth. More broadly, datasheet lines map onto
two distinct damage channels: spectral imperfections (jitter and
longitudinal-mode structure) that degrade the heralded purity, and
statistical imperfections (RIN) that leave the purity exactly
invariant but degrade the pair statistics.

The scope is that of a numerical modeling study: first-order (linear)
GVM phase matching, quasi-static ensemble noise, and heralded spectral
purity (together with the relative double-pair boost) as the figures
of merit. Absolute pair rate, heralding efficiency, and higher-order
dispersion lie outside the model.

This paper makes four contributions.
\begin{enumerate}
\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\item The first quantitative pump-imperfection budget for heralded
  spectral purity with stated Monte-Carlo uncertainties
  (Table~\ref{tab:budget}, Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
\item An explicit datasheet-to-model translation layer, applied to
  three real commercial lasers (Table~\ref{tab:datasheet},
  Sec.~\ref{sec:discussion}).
\item Closed-form external validation of the ensemble pipeline
  against the exact double-Gaussian purity of Eqs.~(\ref{eq:p0})
  and (\ref{eq:pj}) (Sec.~\ref{sec:model}; Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}).
\item A demonstrated $1/L$ scaling law for the jitter tolerances
  (Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling}).
\end{enumerate}
