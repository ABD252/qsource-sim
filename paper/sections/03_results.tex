\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Ideal-source baseline}

We first establish the ideal-pump baseline that all imperfection budgets
refer to. Figure~\ref{fig:design}(a) shows the heralded purity of
Eq.~(\ref{eq:purity}) as a function of the pump bandwidth for the 10~mm
reference crystal. The curve has the broad interior optimum expected
when the pump-envelope and phase-matching correlations of
Eq.~(\ref{eq:jsa}) are balanced \cite{grice1997,law2000,mosley2008}:
the grid-limited peak is $P_{\max} = 0.8106$ at $0.178$~THz on the
sweep's grid ($n = 512$, 6~THz frequency half-window), the top is
flat, and the canonical reference point $\sigma = 0.164$~THz gives
$P = 0.8100$ on the reference grid ($n = 256$, 4~THz half-window) used
for all Monte-Carlo studies below. The 1\% tolerance
window spans 0.159--0.202~THz, so the bandwidth itself is a forgiving
design parameter. The fall-off is nearly log-symmetric about the
optimum: equal factors from the peak give nearly equal purities
(e.g.\ $P = 0.663$ and $0.665$ a factor of $1.9$ below and above it),
and the endpoint values---$P = 0.41$ at $0.05$~THz against $0.24$ at
$1.2$~THz---differ only because the sweep extends further on the
broadband side (factors of $3.6$ and $6.7$ from the peak,
respectively). The residual
gap to $P = 1$ is the familiar penalty of an unapodized sinc
phase-matching function \cite{branczyk2011,graffitti2018}.
Figure~\ref{fig:design}(b) shows the complementary sweep: at fixed
$\sigma = 0.164$~THz the crystal length has its optimum at 10--12~mm
($P = 0.806$ at 10~mm, $0.805$ at 12~mm), falling to $0.286$ at 2~mm
and $0.51$ at 30~mm. This is the same matching condition seen from the
crystal side. The small offset between the $0.806$ quoted here and the
$0.8100$ reference value above is a frequency-window truncation effect,
not discretization: the slowly decaying sinc side lobes make the
computed purity drift with the window half-width ($P = 0.8100$,
$0.8074$, and $0.8061$ at the same operating point for the 4, 6, and
8~THz half-windows of the reference grid, the bandwidth sweep, and the
length sweep, respectively), so ideal purities are comparable only
within a fixed window and are window-independent only at the level
$P \approx 0.81$. As a narrowband anchor, a 5~GHz pump yields
$P = 0.044$ and $K = 22.6$ (Eq.~(\ref{eq:schmidt})) once the
grid resolves the few-GHz pump ridge (which is narrower than the pixel
of the sweep grids), recovering the strongly anti-correlated quasi-CW
limit.

\subsection{Spectral drift and jitter}

Figure~\ref{fig:jitter} shows the heralded purity of the Monte-Carlo
mixed state (Eq.~(\ref{eq:rho})) as the pump center frequency jitters
with increasing rms amplitude, for three length-matched sources. At
zero jitter each ensemble reproduces its ideal value exactly
($P(0) = 0.8100$ for the 10~mm reference), and the seed-mean
degradation is monotonic across the sweep. The shaded bands are the
$\pm 1\sigma$ spread over $S = 16$ independent seeds ($N = 120$
realizations per seed and sweep point); this seed-to-seed scatter
dominates the negligible discretization error ($\sim 2\times 10^{-5}$
under grid refinement at fixed window). Quoting each budget as the
mean $\pm$ standard error of the per-seed threshold crossings, the
10~mm reference crystal tolerates $27.7 \pm 0.3$, $62.7 \pm 0.7$, and
$91 \pm 1$~GHz rms of center jitter for a 1/5/10\% relative purity
drop (in wavelength terms, $27.7$~GHz at 775~nm is $55.5$~pm). The
1\% cell comes from a dedicated fine sweep (5~GHz steps around the
crossing, $N = 240$); an $N$-convergence check on the 10~mm sweep
finds $|b(N{=}240) - b(N{=}120)| < 2\,\mathrm{SEM}$ at every drop
level, so the budgets are unchanged within error bars when $N$
doubles. Re-matching the source with a 5~mm crystal and a
$0.328$~THz pump (ideal $P = 0.8183$) roughly doubles every budget,
to $54.1 \pm 0.9$, $125 \pm 1$, and $181 \pm 3$~GHz, while a 20~mm
crystal matched to a $0.082$~THz pump (ideal $P = 0.8061$) roughly
halves them, to $13.5 \pm 0.2$, $31.3 \pm 0.4$, and
$45.3 \pm 0.6$~GHz: the phase-matching bandwidth scales as $1/L$
(Eq.~(\ref{eq:pm})), so a shorter crystal simply presents a wider
acceptance to a wandering pump, at the cost of a lower pair rate that
our model does not capture. We quantify this scaling below.

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig3_jitter}
\caption{Heralded purity versus pump center jitter/drift (rms, treated
as a quasi-static ensemble) for three length-matched sources: a 20~mm
crystal with a $0.082$~THz pump (triangles), the 10~mm reference
source (circles), and a 5~mm crystal with the pump re-matched to
$0.328$~THz (squares). Shaded bands are the $\pm 1\sigma$ spread over
16 Monte-Carlo seeds; dotted lines mark the respective ideal
purities.}
\label{fig:jitter}
\end{figure}

\subsection{External validation against a closed form}

The Monte-Carlo pipeline can be benchmarked against an exact result.
For Gaussian phase matching the JSA is a two-dimensional Gaussian and
remains one under any rigid center shift, so the purity of the jitter
ensemble takes the closed form
$P(J) = P_0\,[1 + 4c\,(2\pi J)^2]^{-1/2}$ of Eq.~(\ref{eq:pj}), with
$J$ the rms center jitter and $P_0 = 0.9818$ at the reference
operating point. Figure~\ref{fig:validation}(a) overlays the full
Monte-Carlo sweep on this prediction: the $\pm 1\sigma$ band of 16
seeds lies on the curve across the entire sweep, and the budgets
extracted from the Monte-Carlo data by the same threshold machinery
used everywhere else ($30 \pm 0.3$, $68.5 \pm 0.8$, and $102 \pm
1$~GHz for 1/5/10\%) match the closed-form predictions (29.9, 69.0,
and 101.7~GHz) within error bars. This validates the estimator end to
end---sampling, mixing, and threshold extraction---against a result
derived with no Monte-Carlo input. For the sinc model no closed form
exists, but its normalized decay $P(J)/P(0)$ tracks the analytic
shape $g(J) = [1 + 4c\,(2\pi J)^2]^{-1/2}$ with a maximum deviation
of 0.041 over the dense sweep (Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}(b)). We
emphasize what this comparison does and does not show: the Gaussian
model's ideal purity ($P_0 = 0.9818$) differs strongly from the sinc
value ($0.8100$) because the unapodized sinc side lobes carry
entanglement \cite{branczyk2011,graffitti2018}, so panel~(b) compares
decay \emph{shapes} only, never absolute purities. Consistently, the
Gaussian-PM jitter budgets are 8--13\% larger than the sinc budgets
(about 8\% at the 1\% level, e.g.\ $30 \pm 0.3$ against
$27.7 \pm 0.3$~GHz, rising to 13\% at the 10\% level):
the side lobes make the unapodized source slightly more
jitter-sensitive. The ensemble method itself transfers unchanged to
engineered (apodized) crystals \cite{branczyk2011,graffitti2018},
whose phase matching is close to this Gaussian benchmark.

\begin{figure*}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig6_validation}
\caption{External validation against a closed form. (a) Gaussian
phase matching: heralded purity versus center jitter from the full
Monte-Carlo pipeline ($\pm 1\sigma$ band over 16 seeds) against the
closed form $P_0[1 + 4c(2\pi J)^2]^{-1/2}$ of Eq.~(\ref{eq:pj})
(dashed); the dotted line marks the ideal purity $P_0 = 0.9818$.
(b) Shape transfer to the sinc model: normalized decay $P(J)/P(0)$
(seed-mean points) against the analytic shape $g(J)$ (dashed),
with a maximum deviation of 0.041 over the sweep. Panel (b) compares
normalized shapes only: the absolute ideal purities differ ($0.8100$
for sinc against $0.9818$ for Gaussian phase matching).}
\label{fig:validation}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Scaling with crystal length}

The three length-matched jitter sweeps collapse onto a simple design
law. Figure~\ref{fig:scaling} plots the jitter budgets against the
inverse crystal length: the 5\% budgets of $31.3$, $62.7$, and
$125$~GHz at $L = 20$, 10, and 5~mm stand in the ratios
$1 : 2.00 : 4.00$, and through-origin linear fits describe all three
drop levels within twice the seed-to-seed standard errors. This is a
demonstrated $1/L$ law, consistent with the $1/L$ scaling of the
phase-matching bandwidth in Eq.~(\ref{eq:pm}), and it gives the
designer a one-parameter handle: halving the crystal length doubles
every jitter budget.

\begin{figure}[t]
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig7_scaling}
\caption{Jitter budgets versus inverse crystal length for the three
length-matched sources of Fig.~\ref{fig:jitter}. Dashed lines are
linear fits through the origin for each relative-drop level; the 5\%
budgets stand in the ratios $1 : 2.00 : 4.00$ at $L = 20/10/5$~mm,
demonstrating the $1/L$ scaling of the jitter tolerance.}
\label{fig:scaling}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Longitudinal-mode structure}

Multimode pump operation is far less forgiving.
Figure~\ref{fig:multimode}(a) shows the purity ladder for an
equal-weight comb at $0.25$~THz spacing:
$P = 0.810$, $0.671$, $0.543$, $0.445$, $0.377$, $0.330$, $0.292$ for
1--7 lines. A single extra mode at this spacing already costs 17\% of
the purity---more than the entire 10\% budget---so the maximum mode
count within any budget is one. Figure~\ref{fig:multimode}(b) sweeps
the spacing of a fixed 3-mode comb: at $0.02$~THz the lines are
unresolved by the JSA and the purity is essentially the single-mode
value, while at $0.6$~THz it has fallen to roughly $0.31$. The spacing
budgets are $29.6 \pm 0.1$, $75.9 \pm 0.5$, and $112.1 \pm 0.7$~GHz
for 1/5/10\%. The practical reading is that a
nominally single-frequency pump that occasionally hops or lases on a
side mode is a first-order threat to heralded purity, on par with the
strongest jitter considered above.

comb (0.02--0.6~THz); for spacings unresolved by the JSA the

\subsection{Intensity noise attacks a different metric}

Relative intensity noise behaves qualitatively differently
(Fig.~\ref{fig:rin}). Because a pulse-energy fluctuation rescales the
ensemble weights $w_k$ in Eq.~(\ref{eq:rho}) without changing any
normalized spectral amplitude, the heralded purity is exactly
invariant: on the $n = 64$ grid used for this study it is flat at
$0.809956$ for every RIN value from 0 to 30\% rms
(Fig.~\ref{fig:rin}(a)). What RIN does damage is the pair statistics
relevant for heralding fidelity and accidental double pairs
\cite{takeoka2015,meyerscott2020}: the double-pair boost
$\langle w^2 \rangle / \langle w \rangle^2$ follows the small-$r$
prediction $1 + r^2$, with a measured $1.0221$ at 15\% RIN against a
theoretical $1.0225$, and $1.0940$ at 30\% against $1.09$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:rin}(b)). The clipping of the weights
$w = \max[0,\, 1 + \mathcal{N}(0, r^2)]$ at zero shifts the expected
boost by only $\sim 10^{-4}$ even at 30\% RIN (exact clipped value
$1.0899$), so the residual $\pm 0.004$ scatter about $1 + r^2$ is
finite-sample noise of the $N = 10^3$, 8-seed estimator. Budgeted on the
boost ($N = 10^3$ realizations per point, mean $\pm$ SEM over 8
seeds), a $+1\%$ increase is reached at $9.93 \pm 0.08$\% RIN and
$+5\%$ at $22.3 \pm 0.2$\%; $+10\%$ is not reached within the 30\%
sweep. The practical cost is easiest to state through the
coincidences-to-accidentals ratio (CAR). With mean pair number $\mu$
per pulse, true heralded coincidences scale as $\mu$ while accidental
double pairs scale as $\mu^2$, so $\mathrm{CAR} \sim 1/\mu$ in the
standard multi-pair framework \cite{takeoka2015}; RIN multiplies the
double-pair term by the boost, dividing the CAR by it---equivalent to
running the source at $\mu_{\mathrm{eff}} = \mu \times
\mathrm{boost}$. At $\mu = 0.05$, a 30\% RIN (boost $1.0940$) costs
8.6\% of the CAR, equivalent to $\mu_{\mathrm{eff}} = 0.0547$; at the
$+1\%$ budget (9.9\% RIN) the CAR cost is 1\%.

\subsection{The imperfection budget}

Table~\ref{tab:budget} collects the results of
Figs.~\ref{fig:design}--\ref{fig:rin} into a single auto-generated
budget of eight rows. The convention is as follows: each entry is the
largest value of the imperfection for which the heralded purity stays
within a 1/5/10\% \emph{relative} drop of the ideal value of the same
source ($0.8100$ for the 10~mm source, $0.8183$ and $0.8061$ for the
re-matched 5 and 20~mm sources, and $0.9818$ for the Gaussian
phase-matching benchmark); the pump-bandwidth row is instead the
window of bandwidths within the stated drop of the peak, and
RIN---which leaves purity exactly invariant---is budgeted on the
double-pair boost instead. The side-mode row counts the additional
equal-power longitudinal modes at $0.25$~THz spacing that fit each
budget: zero additional side modes fit any budget, since a single
extra mode already costs 17\% of the purity. The hierarchy is
unambiguous: longitudinal-mode structure is the least tolerable
imperfection, center jitter comes next with budgets in the tens of
GHz, and RIN only matters at the ten-percent level and through a
different observable.
