\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The central practical message of this study is that a pump-laser
datasheet splits into two distinct damage channels. Spectral
specifications---center-frequency stability and longitudinal-mode
structure---act on the heralded state itself: each realization
displaces or replicates the pump band across the phase-matching ridge
of Eq.~(\ref{eq:pm}), the ensemble average of Eq.~(\ref{eq:rho}) mixes
inequivalent Schmidt decompositions, and the purity of
Eq.~(\ref{eq:purity}) falls. Statistical specifications---intensity
noise---rescale each realization without changing its spectral shape,
so the heralded purity is mathematically invariant (constant to better
than $10^{-9}$ in our validation tests, Sec.~\ref{sec:model}; see also
the flat sweep of Sec.~\ref{sec:results}), while the
pair-number statistics degrade: the double-pair boost
$\langle w^2 \rangle / \langle w \rangle^2 \approx 1 + r^2$ (exact to
leading order in $r$) inflates
accidental coincidences and erodes heralding fidelity through exactly
the multi-pair channel analyzed in Ref.~\cite{takeoka2015}. Selecting
a pump therefore means checking both columns: a spectrally quiet laser
with high RIN delivers pure but noisy heralds, while an
intensity-stable laser with a wandering center frequency delivers
clean statistics on a mixed state.

The budgets also show where a designer has leverage. Jitter tolerance
scales as $1/L$, a scaling we now demonstrate directly rather than
infer: across crystals of 20, 10, and 5~mm (pump re-matched in each
case) the 5\% budgets of $31.3 \pm 0.4$, $62.7 \pm 0.7$, and
$125 \pm 1$~GHz~rms stand in the ratios $1:2.00:4.00$, and
through-origin linear fits in $1/L$ describe all three drop levels
within twice the seed error (Fig.~\ref{fig:scaling}; uncertainties
throughout are mean $\pm$ SEM over 16 Monte-Carlo seeds, 8 seeds for
the RIN sweep). Halving the
crystal from 10~mm to 5~mm (pump re-matched to 0.328~THz) thus
doubles every budget, from
$27.7 \pm 0.3$/$62.7 \pm 0.7$/$91 \pm 1$~GHz~rms to
$54.1 \pm 0.9$/$125 \pm 1$/$181 \pm 3$~GHz~rms at the 1\%/5\%/10\%
levels (Fig.~\ref{fig:jitter}), at the price of a lower pair rate
that our model does not capture. The ideal purity itself is unchanged by this
trade: the first-order JSA of Eq.~(\ref{eq:jsa}) is invariant under
the rescaling $L \to L/2$, $\sigma \to 2\sigma$ up to a common
dilation of the frequency axes, which leaves the Schmidt
spectrum---and hence the purity---exactly unchanged, and the
slightly higher value quoted for the 5~mm sweep (0.818 versus 0.810)
is a numerical windowing artifact---the fixed frequency window
truncates relatively more of the sinc side lobes of the twice-wider
5~mm JSA---not a physical improvement. Bandwidth, by contrast, is the easy specification:
the 1\% window spans 0.159--0.202~THz, a full width of roughly 26\% of
the reference bandwidth, and the 10\% window spans 0.114--0.281~THz.
Center stability is the hard one: the 1\% budget of
$27.7 \pm 0.3$~GHz~rms corresponds, via Eq.~(\ref{eq:dlam}) at
$\lambda = 775$~nm, to a wavelength stability of only 55.5~pm
($\approx 0.06$~nm). A pump may exceed its nominal bandwidth by tens
of percent with negligible cost---though narrowing below the nominal
value is penalized sooner, because the reference bandwidth sits near
the lower edge of the tolerance window---but its center must hold to
a sub-0.1-nm tolerance.

\subsection*{Reading a datasheet}

The budgets above are stated in the model's units---GHz~rms of center
jitter, THz of amplitude bandwidth, percent rms of intensity
noise---whereas manufacturers print nanometer spectral widths,
temperature and current coefficients, side-mode suppression ratios,
and integrated noise percentages. The translation layer of
Sec.~\ref{sec:model}, Eqs.~(\ref{eq:fwhm})--(\ref{eq:fsr}), closes
this gap, and Table~\ref{tab:datasheet} applies it line by line to
three real products, one from each class of pump laser a designer is
likely to consider. As a worked anchor for the bandwidth line: the
reference $\sigma/2\pi = 0.164$~THz corresponds, via
Eq.~(\ref{eq:fwhm}), to an intensity FWHM of 0.273~THz, i.e.\ 0.55~nm
at 775~nm by Eq.~(\ref{eq:dlam}).

The free-running Fabry--Perot diode (Thorlabs L785P090) is the
cautionary case: its datasheet publishes \emph{none} of the
budget-relevant lines---no spectral width, no mode spacing, no
temperature coefficient, no RIN, no side-mode suppression ratio. The
printed 775--795~nm center wavelength is unit-to-unit selection, not
drift. A Fabry--Perot diode is multimode by construction, and
Sec.~\ref{sec:results} shows that a single additional equal-power
mode already costs 17\% of the purity, more than the entire 10\%
budget, so the class fails before any number is checked. The deeper
lesson is the absence itself: such a laser cannot even be budgeted
from its datasheet, and is unusable for this application without full
external spectral characterization.

The stabilized DFB diode (eagleyard EYP-DFB-0780) is the opposite
extreme: every spectral-stability line it publishes passes with
enormous margin. Its 1~MHz maximum linewidth enters the quasi-static
model only as a 0.5~MHz bound, more than four orders of magnitude
below the $27.7 \pm 0.3$~GHz budget (a factor of
$\sim 2\times 10^{-5}$). Its temperature coefficient,
$d\lambda/dT = 0.06$~nm/K, translates by Eq.~(\ref{eq:dlam}) to
29.9~GHz/K, so the full 1\% jitter budget---read as a uniform drift
via Eq.~(\ref{eq:pp})---equals holding the case temperature to
$\pm 1.6$~K (and, through $d\lambda/dI = 0.003$~nm/mA, the drive
current to $\pm 32$~mA), ordinary servo performance. Its side-mode
suppression of at least 30~dB is a side-line weight of $10^{-3}$, a
negligible admixture. Yet this spectrally exemplary laser is the
wrong tool for the present purpose: a CW pump with MHz linewidth sits
five orders of magnitude below the 0.159--0.202~THz bandwidth window,
and the purity collapses toward the strongly anti-correlated quasi-CW
regime---already $P = 0.04$ at the 5~GHz narrowband anchor of
Sec.~\ref{sec:results}, and falling roughly in proportion to
bandwidth below that. The DFB class
is the wrong choice for pure heralded photons, though precisely the
right one for CW-pumped entanglement sources, where center stability
is what matters.

The picosecond mode-locked oscillator (Spectra-Physics Tsunami) is
the natural class, and its datasheet shows which line binds. At the
short end of its pulse-width range, a 2~ps sech$^{2}$ pulse
(time--bandwidth product 0.315) has an intensity-spectrum FWHM of
0.158~THz, i.e.\ $\sigma/2\pi = 0.095$~THz via Eq.~(\ref{eq:fwhm})
with the sech$^{2}$ spectrum treated as an effective Gaussian,
below the bandwidth window: at the 10~mm reference crystal this alone
gives $P = 0.66$. Bandwidth is the binding line---and a benign one,
because the purity is recovered by re-matching the crystal to
$L \approx 17$~mm rather than by modifying the laser. Every other
line passes trivially: the specified 0.2\%~rms intensity noise gives
a double-pair boost excess of only $4\times 10^{-6}$, and the 80~MHz
repetition-rate comb has teeth far too finely spaced to be resolved
by the JSA.

\subsection*{Extensions and limitations}

Our ideal purity of 0.810 is the familiar ceiling of sinc-shaped
phase matching, set by the side lobes of the unapodized crystal.
Domain engineering and apodization reshape the phase-matching function
toward a Gaussian and raise this ceiling well above
0.81~\cite{branczyk2011,graffitti2018}. Our budgets are
\emph{relative} degradations from whatever ceiling the phase matching
provides, and the Monte-Carlo ensemble method applies unchanged to
engineered crystals: only the sinc factor in Eq.~(\ref{eq:jsa}) need
be replaced. This is now demonstrated rather than asserted: repeating
the full jitter sweep with the amplitude-matched Gaussian phase
matching of Eq.~(\ref{eq:phig}), whose ideal purity is 0.9818, yields
relative budgets of $30 \pm 0.3$/$68.5 \pm 0.8$/$102 \pm 1$~GHz~rms
(Table~\ref{tab:budget}), about 8\% \emph{larger} than the sinc
values at the 1\% level (8--13\% across the three levels), and these
are reproduced exactly, within one standard error, by the closed form
of Eq.~(\ref{eq:pj}), which predicts 29.9/69.0/101.7~GHz
(Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}). The sinc-model budgets quoted above are
therefore mildly conservative for engineered crystals.

The budgets also translate into two-photon interference, but the
translation depends on the architecture. For two \emph{independent}
identical heralded sources the Hong--Ou--Mandel visibility equals the
single-source purity, $V_{\mathrm{HOM}} = P$
\cite{hong1987,mosley2008}, so the jitter budgets above bound the
interference of sources pumped by separate free-running lasers. If
instead both sources are pumped by the \emph{same} laser, the drift
is common-mode: in each trial the two JSAs are translated rigidly
together by the same center shift, every realization retains the
ideal single-source purity, and within this model the shared-drift
HOM visibility stays at the ideal value $P_0$ \emph{exactly},
independent of the jitter amplitude. The reported budgets are
therefore conservative for single-laser multiplexed architectures, in
which center jitter cancels in common mode.

Several limitations bound the present results, each pointing to future
work. The model uses first-order GVM only: $\Delta k$ is linearized,
with no Sellmeier dispersion or higher-order terms, so the
phase-matching ridge is perfectly straight; curvature will matter for
broadband pumps and long crystals. We compute heralded purity only;
heralding efficiency and absolute pair rate are outside the model,
though a complete source assessment must weigh all three
jointly~\cite{meyerscott2020}. The quasi-static treatment of jitter
is no longer an open caveat: its regime of validity is bounded
explicitly in Sec.~\ref{sec:model}---noise slower than the
$\sim$1~ps pump coherence time and faster than the integration
time---and only sub-picosecond noise, which acts coherently within
each pulse, falls outside it on the fast side; drift slower than the
integration time is instead summarized deterministically through the
bounded-excursion conversion of Eq.~(\ref{eq:pp}). An incoherent pump linewidth,
beyond the Gaussian coherent bandwidth, is not yet a separate
mixed-state channel, which is why the Lorentzian linewidth in
Table~\ref{tab:datasheet} enters only as a quasi-static bound.
Similarly, the comb model asserts mutually incoherent lines and
therefore covers free-running multimode diodes only; a mode-locked
pump comb is phase-coherent, so its JSA is a coherent sum---a pure
state---and requires a coherent-sum treatment rather than the mixture
of Eq.~(\ref{eq:rho}). Finally, the treatment is collinear and
single-spatial-mode.

\section{Conclusion}

We have converted pump-laser datasheet specifications into a
quantitative imperfection budget for the heralded spectral purity of
an SPDC source, using a first-order GVM model with Monte-Carlo pump
ensembles, and we have validated the method against a closed-form
double-Gaussian benchmark that reproduces the measured
Gaussian-phase-matching budgets exactly:
$30 \pm 0.3$/$68.5 \pm 0.8$/$102 \pm 1$~GHz measured versus
29.9/69.0/101.7~GHz predicted. The reference source (10~mm crystal,
0.164~THz pump) has ideal purity 0.810. Keeping the purity within
1\%/5\%/10\% of ideal requires center-frequency jitter below
$27.7 \pm 0.3$/$62.7 \pm 0.7$/$91 \pm 1$~GHz~rms (mean $\pm$ SEM
over 16 Monte-Carlo seeds), equivalent at the 1\% level to holding
the pump wavelength to 55.5~pm; the budgets obey a demonstrated $1/L$
law over 5--20~mm crystals (ratios $1:2.00:4.00$), so a 5~mm crystal
doubles them to $54.1 \pm 0.9$/$125 \pm 1$/$181 \pm 3$~GHz~rms at the
cost of pair rate. Single-longitudinal-mode operation is mandatory: a
second equal-power mode at 0.25~THz spacing alone costs 17\% of the
purity, and the mode-spacing budgets for a three-line comb are
$29.6 \pm 0.1$/$75.9 \pm 0.5$/$112.1 \pm 0.7$~GHz. Pump bandwidth is
forgiving, with a 1\% window of 0.159--0.202~THz. Intensity noise
leaves the purity exactly invariant but inflates the double-pair
boost approximately as $1 + r^{2}$; the Monte-Carlo budgets place the
$+1\%$ crossing at $9.93 \pm 0.08\%$ RIN and the $+5\%$ crossing at
$22.3 \pm 0.2\%$. A translation layer maps these budgets onto real
datasheets, and three case studies span the possible outcomes: a
free-running Fabry--Perot diode publishes none of the budget-relevant
lines and fails by construction; a stabilized DFB diode meets the
drift budget by holding $\pm 1.6$~K yet collapses toward the
strongly anti-correlated quasi-CW regime (already $P = 0.04$ at the
5~GHz narrowband anchor) because its MHz linewidth lies five orders
of magnitude below the bandwidth window; and a picosecond mode-locked
oscillator passes every line except bandwidth, which is recovered by
re-matching the crystal length rather than the laser. The rule for
choosing a pump is simple: spectral specifications damage purity,
statistical specifications damage pair statistics, and both columns
of the datasheet must be checked.
